Week 8 - Festival of Economics

This week we attended talks at the Festival of Economics. We were tasked with creating 2 graphs which support/oppose an idea presented at a talk we attended. In the 'Big Data' talk, Arthur Turrell (Dep. Director at ONS Data Science Campus) said that in the recent inflation crisis, prices of low cost goods were increasing faster than general prices. All the data used here was taken from ONS supermarket website scraping. My Google Colab notebook shows how i converted this data into TIDY format.

Comparison of Food Price Changes

This chart shows the percentage change in average prices of a range of items from
Sept 2021 - Sept 2022. We can see that typically lower cost 'essential' goods like
vegetable oil, bread, and pasta increased the most in price, supporting Arthur's argument.

Low-Cost Food Items vs CPIH

When we instead compare a basket of lower cost grocery goods
with the CPIH index (an extended measure of CPI including housing-related
costs), we see that this basket did not increase much faster than CPIH.

Week 10/11 - Machine Learning

In the final 2 weeks we moved onto machine learning (ML) techniques. We studied a range of supervised and unsupervised ML models and discussed their use cases.

Unsupervised Learning - KMeans Clustering

My unsupervised ML example clusters countries based on a range of environmental indicators. I used
dimensionality reduction to create this scatter chart of the countries which shows the difference
between the clustering outcome, and the grouping of the countries by continent. Since each of the 11
dimensions in this dataset decreases with greater environmental impact, countries to the top right of the
graph have the 'best' environmental performance. My Google Colab notebook shows how I used data from the
Environmental Performance Index site to cluster these countries.

Supervised Learning

For the supervised learning task, I used the same dataset, but
here I am testing whether environmental perfromance is a
good predictor of the country continent. My Colab file shows
how I used Support Vector Classification in sklearn to train
the model on 135 countries, and then test the model on
the remaining 45 countries.

Since the model was 71% accurate it shows that environmental
performance is not entirely indicative of continent.
So we have significant environmental performance variation
across continents.
